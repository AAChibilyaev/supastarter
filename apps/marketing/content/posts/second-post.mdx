## The Search Gap That's Costing You Sales

Your best-selling product shows up on page 3. A customer searches "sneakers" and gets zero results because your catalog uses "trainers." Someone types "cheap" and your search treats it as a keyword instead of ignoring it.

These are relevance problems. They're invisible in your analytics until someone correlates zero-result searches with cart abandonment — and when they do, the numbers are always worse than expected.

The good news: relevance problems are solvable without a data science team. Three tools handle 80% of them: **synonyms**, **curations**, and **stopwords**.

## Synonyms: When Customers Say It Differently

A synonym rule tells the search engine to treat two or more terms as equivalent. There are two kinds:

### Equivalent Synonyms (bidirectional)

```json
{
	"synonyms": ["sneakers", "trainers", "kicks", "athletic shoes"]
}
```

When a customer searches "kicks," results for "sneakers" and "trainers" appear alongside literal matches for "kicks." The reverse is also true.

**Use this for:** regional vocabulary differences, brand name variations, industry jargon versus consumer terms.

### One-Way Synonyms (unidirectional)

```json
{
	"root": "laptop",
	"synonyms": ["notebook computer", "portable computer"]
}
```

Searching "laptop" returns results that match "notebook computer" — but searching "notebook computer" does NOT return results for "laptop." This is useful when one term is authoritative and the others are edge cases.

**Use this for:** technical terms that map to a canonical product category, deprecated product names that redirect to current ones.

### Measuring Synonym Impact

In AACsearch Analytics, filter by query terms you've added synonyms for. Compare the zero-result rate before and after deployment. A well-placed synonym set typically reduces zero-result searches by 15–40% for the targeted terms.

## Curations: Manual Override When Algorithms Aren't Enough

Curations let you pin specific products to the top of search results, or exclude products that shouldn't appear for a given query.

### Pinning a Product

Your summer sale has a featured item. You want it first for searches like "sandals," "summer shoes," and "beach":

```json
{
	"id": "summer-sale-curation",
	"query": "sandals",
	"includedHits": [{ "id": "product-1234", "position": 1 }]
}
```

Product 1234 appears at position 1 regardless of relevance score. Everything else ranks normally below it.

### Excluding Products

A product is out of stock but you haven't removed it from the index yet. Or it's discontinued and you don't want it appearing for certain queries:

```json
{
	"query": "running shoes",
	"excludedHits": ["product-5678", "product-9012"]
}
```

### When to Use Curations

Curations are a scalpel, not a paintbrush. Reserve them for:

- **Seasonal promotions** — pin sale items for relevant queries during a campaign
- **New product launches** — ensure visibility for items that don't have review signals yet
- **Safety recalls or legal exclusions** — remove specific products from appearing for certain searches

Don't use curations to compensate for bad indexing. If 50 products need manual pinning, the underlying issue is probably schema or weighting — fix that instead.

## Stopwords: Ignoring Words That Add Noise

Stopwords are terms the search engine ignores entirely. Common English stopwords — "the," "a," "an," "and" — are usually handled automatically. E-commerce sites need custom stopwords for their specific context.

### Common E-commerce Stopwords

```json
{
	"stopwords": ["cheap", "buy", "purchase", "get", "find", "best", "good", "nice"]
}
```

When a customer searches "buy cheap running shoes," the search engine processes "running shoes" — which is what they actually want. Without stopwords, "buy" and "cheap" become keywords that can dilute relevance scoring.

### What NOT to Add as a Stopword

Be careful with price-adjacent terms. "Cheap" is a stopword candidate because it's intent, not product data. "Discount" is trickier — some of your products might literally have "discount" in their name or description. Test before shipping.

### The Stopwords Anti-Pattern

Some teams add competitor brand names as stopwords to avoid showing their products when customers search for a competitor. This is a mistake — customers searching for a competitor are a conversion opportunity, not a problem. Show them your best alternative instead.

## Putting It Together: A Relevance Audit

Run this audit quarterly:

1. **Export your top 100 zero-result queries.** These are your highest-priority synonym candidates.
2. **Check your top 100 queries with a CTR below 5%.** Low CTR on high-volume terms means the wrong products are ranking first — curation candidates.
3. **Review queries where customers add "cheap," "buy," or "get."** Add these to your stopwords list.
4. **A/B test synonym sets** by deploying to one locale or traffic segment first.

## Start Small, Measure Everything

The trap in relevance tuning is doing too much at once and not knowing what moved the needle. Add one synonym set, measure for a week, then add another. Curations have immediate effect — you'll see CTR change within 24 hours of deployment.

Relevance is never "done." New products introduce new vocabulary. Seasonal trends shift what customers search for. A quarterly review keeps you ahead of the drift.
